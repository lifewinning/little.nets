Some Dumb Words About This Project

Is the internet working?

This is a question that seems pretty easy to answer. Open up a browser window, swipe down on your phone to see if Twitter loads--yes, no, the internet's working. But it's a question that I've been thinking really hard about because it is at the heart of a lot of other, harder, questions that people have been asking over the past few years, and with greater urgency in the past year.

Knowing whether or not the internet's working implies a common understanding of how the internet works. In the contexts in which we usually ask is the internet working? "work" means "accessible, in this moment." And we generally don't care that much about how it works when we're trying to access it. We just want it to work. So we accept, endure, and don't question a number of personal risks and annoyances for an internet that, in a very broad sense, works. We use the password-free wifi in coffee shops. We blink through a barrage of ads. Increasingly, we accept network paywalls and subscription services and slouch toward a tiered access future. Is this the internet working?

Infrastructure is a thing people tend to notice when it's not working. Despite the growing ubiquity of network access and networked objects--from the black mirrors we value beyond measure to the wristbands that endlessly measure our movements--something about the network is not working, or no longer works the way it once did or the way we were promised it would a long time ago.

Exactly who made or broke that promise draws attention to another question implicit in whether the internet works: who does the internet work for? Who currently benefits from a privatized, hyper-surveilled, and hyper-monetized internet? Who doesn't? When the value or functionality for a network or a platform is basically determined by its ability to make money, can it retain the intimacy, the political broadcast power, the collapsed contexts and wonderful weirdness that is, for me, crucial to an internet that works?

But that's me talking. That's what I want from the network, and that's how I want to use the network. The artists involved in LittleNets making work on and with alternative wireless networks offer responses to the question what could alternative internets look like? The network can be really good at building intimacies across great distances. What can we build across a space as small as a house on a remote island? What would we put on an internet that is entirely our own? Whatever comes of this monthlong experiment, I hope that you can take those lessons and bring them to other small-yet-great distances--an apartment building, a city block, a neighborhood.

Something about the internet is not working. And if we want that to change, we need to get to work.

—Ingrid Burrington
